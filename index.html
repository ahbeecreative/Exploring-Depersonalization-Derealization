What is DPDR?

Depersonalization and derealization is a type of dissociative condition in which an individual deals with persistent or recurrent feelings of depersonalization and/or derealization. Often involving disruptions of:

* Awareness

* Consciousness

* and, Memory.

It comprises two distinct facets, experienced either individually or concurrently by individuals:

- Depersonalization:

Depersonalization refers to a state in which the sense of self and the quality of subjective first-person experiences are oddly altered. One of its core clinical features is a sense of detachment—a feeling that the self is detached from one's own body.

- Derealization:

This refers to a sense that the things around the individual isn't real. It has been characterized as feeling cut off or out of touch with the world, as if you're living a dream.

A Short History of DPDR

The first time the word depersonalization ever pops up is in 1880 by moral philosopher Henri Frédéric Amiel where he writes in a journal entry about his own feelings of experiencing depersonalization. However it was officially coined medically in 1898 by Ludovic Dugas.

In 1954, Ackner addressed the ambiguity surrounding the symptom by outlining four key aspects: (1) a sense of unreality or unfamiliarity concerning oneself, (2) maintaining awareness without developing delusional thoughts, (3) emotional disturbance leading to a loss of emotional response except for discomfort related to depersonalization, and (4) an unpleasant quality that fluctuates in intensity based on the patient's familiarity with the symptom. Steinberg, in defining depersonalization as one of five primary symptoms of dissociation alongside amnesia, derealization, identity confusion, and identity alteration, established a framework where each of the five dissociative disorders showcases distinct symptom profiles incorporating these core features. Consequently, it becomes imperative to assess depersonalization within the broader context of other dissociative symptoms rather than considering it in isolation. (M. Steinberg)

Symptoms of Depersonalization & Derealization

* The feeling of being detached from one part of the body or the whole body

* Feeling estranged from one's own image when looking in the mirror

* Being unable to control one's movements

* Feeling disconnected from one's surroundings

* Being separated from the world like a veil, or the feeling that the individual is an outside observer

Symptoms of depersonalization/derealization disorder may start gradually or suddenly. Episodes may last for only hours or days or for weeks, months, or years. Episodes may involve depersonalization, derealization, or both. The intensity of symptoms often waxes and wanes. But when the disorder is severe, symptoms may be present and remain at the same intensity for years or even decades.

Causes of DPDR

Depersonalization/derealization disorder often develops in people who have experienced severe stress, including the following:

* Emotional abuse or neglect during childhood

* Physical abuse

* Experiencing or witnessing domestic violence

* Having a severely impaired or mentally ill parent

* Unexpected death of a loved one

Symptoms can be triggered by severe stress (for example, due to relationships, finances, or work), depression, anxiety, or use of illicit drugs. However, in 25 to 50% of cases, the causes of stress are relatively minor or cannot be identified. (Spiegel, D. )

How to Deal with DPDR

Understanding Triggers and Coping Strategies: Identifying triggers and learning effective coping mechanisms are pivotal in managing depersonalization. Recognizing what sets off dissociative episodes is crucial for gaining control and stability.

Minimizing Dissociative States: The primary aim is to reduce or eliminate dissociative episodes through techniques and therapies that emphasize grounding strategies, bringing individuals back to the present moment and enhancing their sense of reality.

Enhancing Relationships: Strengthening interpersonal connections and addressing relational challenges form an integral part of the healing process. Healthy relationships can significantly influence one's self-perception and alleviate depersonalization symptoms.

Managing Co-occurring Mental Health Disorders: Frequently, depersonalization accompanies other mental health conditions. Therapeutic modalities like Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and psychodynamic therapy assist in dismantling unproductive thinking patterns. Family therapy can complement individual sessions, fostering a supportive atmosphere.

Medications for Co-occurring Conditions: While not specifically prescribed for depersonalization, medications such as antianxiety or antidepressants might benefit individuals with concurrent mental health issues.

Holistic Approaches: Holistic methods encompass practices promoting overall well-being: Meditation, yoga, tai chi, acupuncture, music therapy, and art therapy contribute to grounding individuals, fostering mindfulness, and aiding recovery from depersonalization.
